# Skill: Cold Email Copywriting Framework

Used by: Copywriter agent

This skill defines the brand voice, copywriting principles, and sequence frameworks used across all campaigns. All Copywriter outputs are governed by this skill.

---

## Brand Voice

**The core posture**: We reach out because we're genuinely curious about what the other person is building or dealing with. The offer comes later. The relationship comes first.

**Tone descriptors:**
- Human, not corporate
- Curious, not clever
- Warm, not familiar
- Direct, not blunt
- Confident, not pushy

**The mental model for every email**: Write as if you're reaching out to someone you'd genuinely like to have a 20-minute conversation with. You're not selling. You're opening a door.

---

## The Approach: Relationship-First

This business does not open with a pitch. The standard approach is:

1. **First contact**: An observation, question, or invitation, something that shows you've thought about their world. Soft CTA: a research call, a free event, or simply a reply.
2. **Follow-ups**: Add value (an insight, a useful question, a relevant result). Don't just nudge. Give them a reason to respond.
3. **The offer**: Introduced naturally over the sequence, not forced, not hidden, but not the opening line.

This works because:
- It screens for people who are genuinely open, not just politely tolerating a pitch
- It creates goodwill even with people who don't convert now
- It matches how this business earns clients: through trust, not pressure

---

## Sequence Length and Timing: Defaults

| Step | Name | Send Day | Length Target |
|------|------|----------|---------------|
| Email 1 | Warm Opener | 1 | 60-100 words |
| Email 2 | Value Add | 5 | 50-80 words |
| Email 3 | Different Angle | 12 | 60-100 words |
| Email 4 | Breakup | 20 | 30-50 words |

---

## Email 1: Warm Opener

**Goal**: Get a reply or a click to a low-friction CTA. Not a sale.

**Structure:**
1. The `{{hook}}` (the Personaliser inserts this, leave the placeholder)
2. One sentence connecting their world to the reason you're reaching out
3. A soft CTA: "Would you be open to a 20-minute call?", "We're running a small event on [topic], worth a seat?" or simply "Would love your take on [question]."

**Subject line formulas that work:**
- "[First name] - quick question"
- "A thought on [specific topic from their world]"
- "Something that might be useful for [company]"
- "[First name] - [short observation]"
- "Re: [topic they'd care about]" (only if genuinely relevant, not deceptive)

**What to avoid:**
- "I wanted to reach out..." (never start here)
- "My name is X and I help companies like yours..." (pitch-opening)
- Fake personalisation: "As a fellow [industry] professional..." (no one finds this compelling)
- Long intros about the company before saying anything useful

---

## Email 2: Value Add

**Goal**: Give them a reason to engage, not just a reminder that you exist.

**What to add:**
- A useful insight about their industry or role
- A question that makes them think
- A resource: article, framework, tool (genuinely relevant, not self-promotional)
- A result from someone in a similar position

**Subject line approach**: Don't reference Email 1 directly. Fresh subject that connects to what you're sharing.

**What to avoid:**
- "Just following up on my last email" (pure noise, offers nothing)
- Any variation of "I wanted to check in" (offers no value)

---

## Email 3: Different Angle

**Goal**: Give them a third reason to engage. Use a different angle than Emails 1 and 2.

**Angle options:**
- A short case study or result (one specific example, concrete outcome)
- A reframe: show the problem from a different direction
- A provocative question about how they currently handle X

**Subject line approach**: Treat this as a fresh email. Don't reference the sequence.

---

## Email 4: Breakup

**Goal**: Leave on a warm note. Close the loop without guilt or pressure.

**Structure:**
- Acknowledge this is the last one
- Remove all pressure, explicitly
- Leave the door open for when the time is right
- One sentence: how to reach you if they ever want to

**What it should NOT do:**
- Express frustration or passive aggression
- Use false scarcity ("last chance")
- Make them feel guilty for not responding

**Example tone**: "Last one from me. Clearly the timing isn't right, and that's completely fine. If anything changes, you know where to find me."

## Universal Rules

**Never use em dashes (—) anywhere in copy.** They are a tell-tale sign of AI-generated writing. Replace with: a comma, a period, a colon, or restructure the sentence entirely.

**Words and phrases to avoid in any cold email:**
- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "I wanted to reach out"
- "We help companies like yours"
- "Quick question" (as a subject line trick, only use if it's genuinely a question)
- "Leverage", "synergy", "streamline", "unlock", "revolutionise", "game-changer"
- "At scale", "move the needle"
- Any bullet-pointed list of features in a cold email
- Formal sign-offs like "Best regards" (use first name or "Thanks")

**Structural rules:**
- No bullet points in Email 1 (conversational, not a deck)
- One CTA per email, never two
- Sign every email with sender first name only
- No logos, images, or rich formatting (plain text converts better for cold outreach)
- Include an unsubscribe opt-out line in every email: "If this isn't relevant, just let me know and I'll stop."
