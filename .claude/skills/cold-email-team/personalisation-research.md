# Skill: Personalisation Research Methodology

Used by: Researcher agent

This skill defines what makes a strong personalisation hook, how to research prospects efficiently, and how to decide when you have enough.

---

## What Makes a Good Hook

A good hook has three qualities:

1. **Specific** — it could only apply to this person, not a type of person
2. **Timely** — ideally references something from the last 3-6 months
3. **Relevant** — connects to why we're reaching out, even if indirectly

A hook that passes all three: *"Saw your post about scaling without a sales team — given what you're building at [company], I had a thought worth sharing."*

A hook that fails: *"I noticed you're the CEO of [company] and thought you might be interested in..."*

---

## Research Priority Order

Work through these in order. Stop when you have a Strong hook — don't over-research.

### 1. LinkedIn (fastest, highest signal)
- Recent posts (last 90 days): topics they engage with, opinions they share
- Role tenure: how long in this position? (< 12 months = in transition, worth noting)
- Recent career move: new role = new priorities, often open to new solutions
- Content they comment on (shows what they care about)

### 2. Company News
- Funding: recent raise = growth mode, hiring, new pressures
- Hiring activity: what roles are open? (signals pain and priorities)
- Product launches, expansions, rebrands
- Leadership changes (new CXO = new strategy = new buying criteria)
- Press coverage, award wins, partnerships

### 3. Tech Stack
- Job postings often list tools they use (e.g. "experience with HubSpot, Salesforce...")
- G2, Capterra, Crunchbase sometimes show integrations
- Only relevant if the offer relates to their tech decisions

### 4. Personal Signals
- Podcast appearances or interviews (what did they say?)
- Conference talks (recent = high signal)
- Published articles or thought leadership
- Mutual connections or shared background

---

## Hook Quality Rating Criteria

**Strong** — use this:
- References something specific and recent
- Shows you actually looked at this person, not just their title
- Connects naturally to the reason for reaching out

**Medium** — use if nothing stronger is available:
- Relevant and true, but not highly specific (e.g. references a company news event but not the person directly)
- Could apply to a few people at the company, not just this one

**Weak** — flag, do not pass through unchallenged:
- Generic industry reference
- Role-based assumption with no specific evidence
- Could apply to hundreds of people with this title

---

## What "Enough Research" Looks Like

Stop researching a prospect when you have:
- One Strong hook, OR
- Two Medium hooks (combine the strongest elements)

Do not spend more than 5-10 minutes per prospect. If you can't find a Strong hook in that time, rate it Medium or Weak and move on — flagging it is better than making something up.

**Never fabricate or assume.** If you can't find a specific signal, write what you found accurately and rate it accordingly.

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Title-baiting**: "As a VP of Sales, you probably deal with..." — this is not research
- **Company size observation**: "I see [company] is a growing startup..." — generic
- **Congratulating on a role they've had for 3 years**: check tenure before writing "Congratulations on the new role"
- **Outdated news**: using a funding round from 2021 in 2025 — check dates
- **Copying LinkedIn headlines verbatim** — they know what their own headline says

---

## Research Brief Quality Check

Before marking a brief complete, ask:
- Would the prospect recognise this hook as being written specifically for them?
- Is every fact true and verifiable?
- Is the hook dated within the last 6 months (or is it clearly evergreen)?
- Does the hook connect — even loosely — to why we're reaching out?

If any answer is no, revise or rate down.
